I started my career as a freelance developer a long time ago in high school. Although I was more interested in the field of information security than in simple development, I couldn't afford to start working in a specialty due to demands of the industry.

At the same time, I managed to establish my first outstaff team. I, at the head of a group of 5 members, started to slowly enter the cybersecurity field. Unfortunately, due to lack of experience and competition, we didn't get very far, but it was a great first experience.
It was around that time that I realized I was going to become a white hacker and engineer.

<=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=->



At university I started working as a developer, which I don't like to talk about as I consider it a rough period in my life. Although first jobs have their advantages besides PTSD, because now I can bravely write about stress tolerance :) Nevertheless, this job gave me a great understanding of the business processes of building products and gave me the opportunity to try myself as a versatile developer, from back&front-end to cyber security. After leaving that place, I finally decided that I needed to continue growing as a hacker.


In my first year, Me and my comrade decided to organize ITHub, due to the absence of such associations in the university. Acting as a leader, not without the help of my comrades, led the club to a peak of 100 people and great achievements! We had our own hackathons, both external and internal, we tried our best to help the members of the club to realize their projects and helped a lot of people. We cooperated with the government, performing the most difficult projects, made social networks and video games, as well as tried to entertain the community by organizing a lot of events. Educated students and highschoolers.

However, although we received help from the scientific staff of the university, for which I am grateful to them, the management did not consider it profitable. So after my departure, the club, unfortunately, fell into disrepair.


In addition, by combining several big tasks, mostly described above, I was lucky enough to create a group of people interested in cyber security on the Internet, with whom we have come a long way. All of this grew into a large team, with which I, as an organizer, entered the cybersecurity industry. For a few years we helped a lot of companies around the world, created a bunch of interesting products and at that point I couldn't have dreamed of more. 

<=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=->



The turning point was the day I decided to go formal due to some external circumstances. Even though the employers of the job offered less at the start than I was getting at the time in the outstaff, I realized that it was a worthwhile investment in my career, future, and skills. 
By getting a job as a pentester on a great team, I gained a huge amount of product experience. I was lucky enough to collaborate with huge companies, tested super cool projects, and developed my own. After the evolution into a devsecops, which inevitably happens to any good pentester, I became a chief engineer and product owner at a top-tier corp on the Eurasian continent.


Now I create some of the coolest cybersecurity products with an incredibly talented team and realize that my profession has no limits!

 
Even though I was officially employed as a pentester at my first job, I mostly had to perform the functions of a white-hat hacker, as the companies I worked with asked me to do precisely these things.
 
I have to thank my employer for not forbidding me to do third-party projects while working for him, as it allowed me to grow as a specialist much faster. 

 
I had to hack large corporations and medium-sized businesses by myself most often with the most sophisticated methods, from simple social engineering to creating scenarios of complete collapse of the company because of a custom ransomware. Staff counseling after a successful attack was included in the contract. I don't see the point in talking about ordinary vulnerabilities and their search, as it has already become a base and any senior engineer realizes that this is only the beginning of the way to work on the company, and most of it is not in this. DDOS attacks, direct direct hacking followed by escalation of access rights, exploitation of backdoors installed in the long term, direct access to serverrooms and database leaks. Although I can do a lot of things, I have a lot to aspire to and I try to develop every day.

<=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=-=->



Interesting facts:


My longest long term contract with ISP Corporation lasted three years, during that time they were never once hacked, unlike their competitors. Proud of that.

 
I am working actively on BugBounty.

Bucking refused to pay me the negotiated fee for the vulnerability I found. Last year their clients lost tens of millions of dollars because of it. Great work, the people who got scammed will thank you for your negligence.


Managers of a major bank in my country didn't take my words about a possible leak of their database seriously and called security because I called them idiots. Two months after the incident they lost the entire database. I don't see the reason to describe the obvious consequences.

 
At the end of 2023 I had an anniversary, I helped the police find over 100 internet criminals. Kiss :*


In addition to cybersecurity, I've created a videogames (unsuccessfully), mobile apps, made robots, building drones and pretty good at electrical engineering, circuitry, and soldering. I've also been sereously electrocuted about 15 times.
